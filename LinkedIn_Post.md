**Handy is finally out in the open. No paywall. No waitlist. Completely open source.**

For context — every time I was trying to learn something new on my Mac (modelling an alien in Blender one weekend, figuring out Xcode for the first time the next), my workflow looked like this:

Take a screenshot. Switch tab. Upload to ChatGPT. Read the reply. Switch back. Hunt for the button. Can't find it. Go back. Ask again.

Every single step.

The AI already knew what to do. I knew what I wanted. The gap between us was just Alt-Tab.

So we built Handy.

It's a native macOS assistant that lives in your menu bar. Sees what you see. Listens when you want it to. Talks back. And the part I'm most proud of — it actually understands *where* you are.

A few things that make it feel different:

→ **Voice and chat, together.** Push-to-talk with Ctrl+Z, or open the chat panel with Shift+Space+O. Same assistant, whichever mode fits the moment — speak when your hands are busy, type when you want to think.

→ **A tiny floating widget.** A small pill that quietly sits on the edge of your screen. One tap to open chat. Drag it anywhere. It's there when you want it, invisible when you don't. Completely optional — turn it off if you prefer just the menu bar.

→ **App-aware memory.** When you switch from Xcode to Linear to a GitHub tab, Handy switches conversations with you. Each app — and for browsers, each *website* — gets its own thread. No more hunting through one giant chat blob going "wait, where did I discuss that build error?" One brain, many notebooks.

→ **Visual pointing.** Instead of a wall of text, Handy can literally point to the right button on your screen with a small companion cursor — while the longer explanation sits quietly in chat for you to read later. Short answer for the eye, detailed answer for the brain.

→ **Local-first by design.** Your chat history stays on your Mac as plain files. Your API keys live in the system Keychain. No Handy cloud, no backend collecting your screenshots. Your stuff stays yours.

→ **Optional web search.** When turned on, the model itself decides whether your question actually needs fresh info from the internet — so there's zero slowdown on the 80% of questions that don't need it. The other 20%, it goes and fetches real-time answers on its own.

And the part that matters most to me — **Handy is completely open source.**

This project exists because of an open source repo that inspired us. Felt only fair to give it back the same way. No gatekeeping. Take it, fork it, break it, improve it, ship your own version of it. If more people build on top of it, we all win.
